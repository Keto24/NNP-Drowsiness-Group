Introduction Outline

1. Introduction: The increasing number of accidents caused by drowsy driving poses a major risk to road safety.

2. Background: Existing solutions, such as lane detection and steering monitoring, often fail to detect early signs of drowsiness.

3. Transition: Our approach leverages computer vision and neural networks to analyze real-time facial data for more accurate drowsiness detection.

4. Details: The project involves facial feature extraction using OpenCV, real-time classification using neural networks, and an alert system.

5. Assessment: Success will be measured by achieving an 85%+ accuracy rate, minimizing false positives, and ensuring real-time performance.

Ethical Sweep

General Questions:

  • Should we even be doing this? Yes, as drowsiness detection improves road safety and prevents accidents.
  • What might be the accuracy of a non-ML alternative? Traditional methods, like motion sensors or steering detection, have lower accuracy and do not consider facial fatigue indicators.
  • What processes will we use to handle appeals/mistakes? The system should allow users to disable or calibrate sensitivity to reduce unnecessary alerts.
  • How diverse is our team? Our team consists of members from different backgrounds, which helps reduce bias and improve inclusivity.

Data Questions:

  • Is our data valid for its intended use? We will use publicly available datasets and conduct additional validation.
  • What bias could be in our data? Bias may exist in skin tone detection, facial structure variation, and lighting conditions.
  • How could we minimize bias in our data and model? We will include diverse datasets with a range of ethnicities, lighting conditions, and face shapes.
  • How should we audit our code and data? Regular testing and comparison with existing models to check for fairness.

Impact Questions:

  • Do we expect different error rates for different sub-groups? Yes, and we will evaluate performance across different demographics.
  • What are likely misinterpretations of results? Users might rely too heavily on the system, thinking it is 100% accurate.
